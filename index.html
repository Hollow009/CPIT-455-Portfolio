WEEK 03–04

CLASS 01 · Domain Spine: Dependability

Topics: Availability, Reliability, Safety, Security, Resilience

🎯 ATM Dependability Analysis

This activity focused on analyzing the dependability of an ATM system and understanding how different engineering principles work together to ensure secure and reliable banking operations.

🔹 Dependability Attributes

  • Availability: The ATM should operate continuously so customers can access services anytime.
  • Reliability: Transactions must complete correctly without balance or withdrawal errors.
  • Safety: The system should prevent financial loss and incorrect transaction behavior.
  • Security: Customer information and PINs must remain encrypted and protected.
  • Maintainability: Failures should be diagnosed and repaired quickly to reduce downtime.
  • Integrity: Transaction records must remain accurate and protected from modification.

⚠️ Risk Identification

Hardware Risks

  • Card reader malfunction
  • Cash dispenser jam
  • Power failure
  • Touchscreen or keypad damage

Software Risks

  • Transaction processing bugs
  • Operating system crashes
  • Database synchronization failures
  • Software update errors

Operational Risks

  • Incorrect technician configuration
  • Poor maintenance scheduling
  • Physical vandalism
  • Internet connectivity interruptions

🛡 Dependability Strategy

Redundancy

  • Backup communication network
  • Duplicate banking servers

Testing

  • Unit testing for transactions
  • Integration testing with servers
  • Stress testing during peak usage

Security Protection

  • Encrypted communication
  • Firewall protection
  • Secure PIN encryption modules

Monitoring & Recovery

  • Real-time system monitoring
  • Automatic reboot after crashes
  • Transaction rollback mechanisms

📘 Learning Outcome

This task helped me understand that dependability extends beyond basic functionality. Availability, reliability, security, and integrity work together to build user trust in critical systems such as ATMs.

⚡ Challenges

At first, distinguishing between hardware, software, and operational risks was difficult. Breaking the ATM system into components helped clarify how failures can originate from both technical and human factors.

💡 Key Insight

Failures cannot always be completely prevented, but systems can be designed to tolerate them. Redundancy and recovery mechanisms help maintain safe transactions even during unexpected failures.

🌍 Real-World Connection

ATM systems are mission-critical banking systems where failures directly impact users financially. Dependability engineering helps ensure continuous service, secure transactions, and accurate financial operations.

✅ Conclusion

This activity demonstrated how structured risk analysis and dependability strategies are essential for designing secure and reliable real-world systems.

WEEK 05–06

CLASS 02 · Reliability Engineering

Topics: Faults, Errors, Failures, Reliability Metrics

🚚 Uber Eats Delivery System Justification

This activity focused on analyzing the dependability requirements of a real-world food delivery platform and understanding how availability and scalable architecture support continuous service.

🖼 System Diagram

Figure: Delivery system architecture and workflow diagram.

🌍 Real-World System: Uber Eats Food Delivery Platform

Uber Eats is used by three main groups: customers, restaurants, and delivery drivers. Customers browse menus, place orders, and track deliveries through the application, while restaurants and drivers interact with the platform to complete the delivery process.

The system experiences dynamic traffic patterns, especially during lunch hours, dinner hours, weekends, and special events where thousands of requests may occur simultaneously.

Users expect fast response times, smooth checkout processes, and real-time delivery tracking, which makes system scalability and dependability extremely important.

📊 Chosen Dependability Metric: Availability

Availability was selected as the most important dependability metric because Uber Eats operates as an on-demand platform where users expect immediate and continuous access.

If the system becomes unavailable, customers cannot place orders, restaurants lose sales opportunities, and drivers lose delivery requests.

Measuring uptime percentage directly reflects both user satisfaction and business performance.

🏗 Proposed Dependability Strategy: Microservices Architecture

Independent Services

Core functions such as ordering, payment processing, and delivery matching operate independently instead of relying on one monolithic system.

Failure Isolation

If one service temporarily fails, other services can continue functioning normally, reducing the impact on the entire platform.

Scalability

Services can scale separately during peak traffic periods, helping the platform maintain stable performance.

Maintainability

Independent deployment allows developers to update or repair services quickly without shutting down the whole system.

📘 Learning Outcome

This task helped me understand how software architecture decisions directly affect system dependability, scalability, and user experience.

⚡ Challenges

One challenge was understanding why availability is prioritized over other dependability metrics in food delivery systems. Analyzing real-world usage patterns clarified the importance of continuous service accessibility.

💡 Key Insight

Modern large-scale applications rely heavily on distributed architectures because they improve fault tolerance and scalability.

🌍 Real-World Connection

Platforms such as Uber Eats must maintain high availability because even short periods of downtime can directly impact revenue, customer trust, and operational efficiency.

CLASS 03 · Safety Engineering

Topics: Hazard Analysis, ALARP, Fault Tree Analysis, Safety Cases

💉 Insulin Pump Safety Argument

This activity focused on analyzing safety risks in a medical insulin pump system and understanding how safety requirements help prevent dangerous system behavior.

🏥 System Overview

Insulin Pump Dose Delivery Controller

An insulin pump automatically delivers insulin doses to diabetic patients based on programmed dosage settings and sensor readings.

Since insulin directly affects blood glucose levels, any system failure may create severe medical risks for patients.

⚠️ Hazard Identification

Hazard: Insulin Overdose

The system may deliver insulin beyond the medically safe limit, causing severe hypoglycemia which can lead to brain damage or even death.

This hazard directly threatens patient safety because insulin impacts blood glucose levels immediately.

📊 Risk Assessment Matrix

Hazard Probability Severity Risk Level Verdict
Insulin Overdose Medium Catastrophic High Intolerable

📝 Explanation

  • Probability: Sensor or software failures are uncommon but still possible.
  • Severity: Extremely dangerous because overdose may become fatal.
  • Risk Level: High-risk situations require strict preventive safety mechanisms.

🛡 Safety Requirement

SR1: The system shall not deliver an insulin dose exceeding the medically configured maximum limit under any operating condition.

Justification: Functional requirements define what a system should do, while safety requirements define what a system must never do in order to protect users from harm.

📘 Learning Outcome

This activity helped me understand the importance of safety engineering in medical and safety-critical systems where failures can directly affect human lives.

⚡ Challenges

One challenge was distinguishing between normal functional requirements and safety requirements. I learned that safety requirements focus specifically on preventing dangerous outcomes.

💡 Key Insight

Even highly reliable systems can still be unsafe if dangerous failures are not properly controlled through safety requirements and risk analysis.

🌍 Real-World Connection

Medical systems such as insulin pumps require extremely strict safety controls because software failures may lead to severe injury or death.

Security & Resilience and Software Reuse

🛡 Security, Resilience Engineering & Software Reuse

This phase focused on understanding how modern software systems remain secure, resilient, and maintainable even during failures, cyberattacks, and unexpected operational conditions.

I also learned how software reuse techniques help organizations accelerate development while improving system dependability and reducing costs.

🔐 Security & Resilience Engineering

Security Engineering

Security engineering focuses on protecting systems from unauthorized access, attacks, and data breaches. Reliable security mechanisms are essential for protecting user trust and sensitive information.

Resilience Engineering

Resilience engineering focuses on designing systems that continue operating even during failures, disruptions, or cyberattacks.

Human-in-the-Loop Systems

Human operators supervise AI systems and intervene when necessary, ensuring human oversight in critical situations.

Explainable AI (XAI)

Explainable AI improves transparency in AI decision-making, helping engineers identify issues and increase trust in intelligent systems.

📈 Industry Trends in Resilience Engineering

Chaos Engineering

Companies such as Netflix intentionally introduce failures into systems to test resilience and recovery capabilities.

Microservices Architecture

Organizations like Amazon use microservices to isolate failures and improve scalability and recovery.

Cloud Computing

Cloud platforms provide built-in resilience features such as redundancy, backup systems, and disaster recovery mechanisms.

AIOps

AI for IT Operations automates monitoring and incident response, improving operational resilience and recovery speed.

♻️ Software Reuse

Software reuse is the process of using existing software components, frameworks, services, or applications to build new systems more efficiently.

Benefits

  • Faster development time
  • Lower development costs
  • Reduced project risks
  • Improved dependability
  • Better standards compliance

Challenges

  • Component adaptation difficulties
  • Maintenance complexity
  • Lack of tool support
  • Compatibility issues
  • Not-Invented-Here syndrome

🚀 Software Reuse Approaches

Application Frameworks

Frameworks provide reusable architectures that support system development through predefined components, security support, and database integration.

Software Product Lines

Product lines allow organizations to build multiple related applications using a shared architecture and reusable components.

Application System Reuse

Existing large-scale systems can be adapted and configured for new customers without changing the entire source code.

AI-Based Reuse

Modern AI tools help identify reusable code, reusable AI models, and reusable software components automatically.

⚠️ Real-World Reuse Failure Example

Ariane 5 Rocket Failure

The Ariane 5 rocket failed because software reused from the Ariane 4 rocket could not handle the new system’s higher speed, causing an overflow error.

This example showed that reused software must always be carefully validated before deployment in new environments.

📘 Learning Outcome

This topic helped me understand how resilience, security, and software reuse are connected in modern software engineering.

⚡ Challenges

One challenge was understanding how reused components can improve development speed while also introducing compatibility and maintenance risks.

💡 Key Insight

Dependable systems are not only secure, but also resilient enough to continue operating during unexpected failures and disruptions.

🌍 Real-World Connection

Modern companies such as Netflix, Amazon, Microsoft, and Google rely heavily on resilience engineering, cloud systems, and reusable architectures to maintain reliable large-scale services.

WEEK 11–12

Distributed Systems & Architecture

Topics: Distributed Systems, Component-Based Software Engineering

🧩 Component-Based & Distributed Software Engineering

This phase focused on understanding how modern software systems are built using reusable components and distributed architectures to improve scalability, flexibility, reliability, and maintainability.

I learned how large systems are separated into smaller independent modules, services, and distributed nodes that work together while remaining scalable and fault tolerant.

🧩 Component-Based Software Engineering (CBSE)

Core Idea

Component-Based Software Engineering focuses on building systems using reusable software components instead of developing everything from scratch.

Benefits

  • Faster development
  • Reduced costs
  • Improved maintainability
  • Better software reuse
  • Higher development efficiency

Component Validation

Components must be validated carefully to ensure compatibility, correct functionality, and protection against malicious behavior.

Trust Issues

Engineers must ensure component suppliers are trustworthy because reused components may introduce security risks or unwanted functionality.

⚙️ Component Composition

Component composition is the process of combining components to create complete software systems.

Sequential Composition

Components execute in sequence. Example: Product selection → payment → checkout.

Hierarchical Composition

One component uses services provided by another component within the system hierarchy.

Additive Composition

Multiple interfaces combine together to create a larger integrated component.

Glue Code & Adaptors

Glue code and adaptor components resolve incompatibilities between components that use different formats or interfaces.

🌐 Distributed Software Engineering

Distributed software systems consist of multiple independent computers connected through networks that work together as one system.

Transparency

Users should not notice that the system is distributed across multiple machines or locations.

Scalability

Distributed systems must handle increasing workloads and users efficiently without major performance loss.

Fault Tolerance

The system should continue functioning even when individual nodes or services fail.

Concurrency

Multiple operations can execute simultaneously across different nodes, improving performance and efficiency.

🏗 Distributed Architecture Patterns

Client–Server Architecture

Clients request services from centralized servers. Common in web applications, online banking, and cloud services.

Multi-Tier Architecture

Systems are divided into layers such as presentation, application, and database layers to improve scalability and organization.

Peer-to-Peer Systems

Nodes share resources directly without centralized control, improving decentralization and resource sharing.

Distributed Components

Components can be deployed independently across different servers and locations, supporting flexible scaling and maintenance.

☁️ Modern Distributed Technologies

Cloud Computing

Cloud platforms provide scalable infrastructure, redundancy, and on-demand resources for distributed systems.

Software as a Service (SaaS)

Applications are delivered over the Internet without requiring local installation or maintenance.

Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA)

Systems are structured as independent stateless services that communicate through standard protocols.

AI & Distributed Systems

Distributed computing is essential for training and deploying large AI models that require massive computational resources.

⚠️ Challenges in Distributed Systems

  • Lack of centralized control
  • Unpredictable network behavior
  • Complex fault recovery
  • Security risks across connected systems
  • Scalability and synchronization difficulties

📘 Learning Outcome

This topic helped me understand how modern large-scale applications are built using reusable components and distributed architectures to improve flexibility, scalability, and reliability.

⚡ Challenges

One challenge was understanding how distributed systems maintain communication and synchronization between independent nodes and services.

💡 Key Insight

Large software systems become more manageable when divided into reusable components and distributed services that can scale independently.

🌍 Real-World Connection

Modern companies such as Amazon, Google, Netflix, and Microsoft rely heavily on distributed architectures, cloud systems, and reusable software components to support millions of users worldwide.

WEEK 13–14

Systems of Systems SOS

Topics: Systems of Systems, Verification & Validation

🚨 Systems of Systems (SoS) Engineering

This activity focused on understanding how independently managed systems collaborate together to achieve larger organizational goals, and the engineering challenges involved when centralized control does not fully exist.

I learned how Systems of Systems (SoS) differ from traditional monolithic systems and why integration, communication, and resilience become critical in large-scale environments.

🌍 Real-World System: Jeddah Smart City Emergency Command Center

911 Emergency Dispatch Integration

The emergency command center integrates multiple independent systems across Jeddah to coordinate emergency response operations more efficiently.

  • Police CAD Systems: Managed by local law enforcement agencies.
  • Hospital Information Systems: Used to track emergency room capacity and bed availability.
  • Traffic Control Networks: Managed by the municipality to control traffic lights and monitoring cameras.

Each subsystem operates independently with its own management, funding, and operational goals, but they collaborate during emergencies to improve ambulance routing and emergency response times.

📊 Systems Comparison Matrix

Characteristic Monolithic System System of Systems (SoS) Notes
Control Centralized Managerial Independence Each system has separate ownership and funding.
Functionality Unified Purpose Operational Independence Components continue operating independently even if the SoS fails.
Development Planned Versions Evolutionary Development Subsystems evolve independently over time.

🏗 Proposed SoS Strategy

Standardized Interfaces

Strong APIs and standardized data exchange mechanisms allow independent systems to communicate effectively without rewriting the original systems.

Adaptability

The SoS architecture must tolerate unexpected updates because component systems evolve independently on different schedules.

Governance Model

Collaboration agreements between departments are more effective than forcing centralized technical control.

Resilience

The architecture must continue functioning even when individual systems fail, disconnect, or become temporarily unavailable.

📘 Learning Outcome

This task helped me understand that Systems of Systems engineering focuses heavily on integration, coordination, and managing relationships between independent systems rather than developing entirely new software from scratch.

⚡ Challenges

One major challenge in SoS engineering is handling emergent behavior, where the combined system may produce unexpected issues that do not appear in the individual subsystems alone.

Testing these environments is difficult because recreating a complete real-world SoS inside a testing laboratory is often impractical.

💡 Key Insight

In Systems of Systems, engineers never have complete control over every subsystem. Therefore, the architecture must remain resilient to independent updates, failures, and operational changes.

🌍 Real-World Connection

Smart city infrastructures, emergency response systems, intelligent traffic management, and healthcare coordination platforms all rely heavily on Systems of Systems engineering to share data securely and reliably across independent organizations.
